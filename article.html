The Forgotten Villages of Northern Morocco

By Elena Vasquez · April 10, 2026

Article hero image

The road narrows as you leave the main highway. Paved road gives way to packed dirt, and the dust rises in small clouds behind the car. This is the northern edge of Morocco's Atlas Mountains, a region that most travel guides mention only briefly before directing readers back toward Marrakech or Fez. But it is here, in the small villages clinging to rocky hillsides, that something remarkable still exists: a way of life that has not fundamentally changed in centuries.

I first came to this area by accident. I had been traveling through Morocco for three weeks, following the usual circuit of medinas and riads, when a local shopkeeper in Chefchaouen mentioned his home village to the east. "Nobody goes there," he said, which is precisely the kind of statement that makes a traveler's ears perk up.

The Village of Ain Tissimane

Ain Tissimane is not on most maps. It sits at roughly 1,400 meters elevation, a collection of about 60 stone houses grouped around a central well. The name roughly translates to "Spring of the Forty" in the local Amazigh dialect, a reference to a legendary group of ancestors who supposedly founded the settlement. The spring itself still runs, channeled through a small stone trough where women fill plastic containers every morning.

The village has no guesthouse. I was welcomed into the home of a farmer named Ahmed and his family after his teenage son, who spoke some French from school in the nearest town, acted as a translator. We communicated mainly through gestures, shared meals, and the universal language of showing photographs on a phone screen.

"The village has no guesthouse, no wifi, no tourist infrastructure of any kind — and that absence is exactly what makes it worth visiting."

The family lives in three rooms. There is a main room with cushions along the walls where they eat, sleep, and receive guests. There is a small kitchen with a gas burner. There is a room for the animals — two goats and a donkey — that also serves as storage. The roof is where life happens on warm evenings, with a view of the valley that stretches toward a horizon of purple mountains.

Daily Life

I stayed for three days. The rhythm of life is tied completely to the land and the animals. Ahmed rises before dawn to check on the goats. His wife Fatima bakes bread in a clay oven built into the courtyard wall. The four children, ranging from about six to sixteen, walk two kilometers to school each day.

There is no wifi. There is a single cell tower visible on a distant ridge that provides intermittent signal. Ahmed has a smartphone but uses it mainly for calls and occasional WhatsApp messages from relatives who have moved to cities. He told me through his son that he had no interest in leaving. "Everything I need is here," he said, gesturing toward the view.

This kind of statement is easy to romanticize. I tried not to. The life is physically hard. Water must be carried. Healthcare requires a long drive to the nearest town. The income from farming and a few goats is modest. And yet there is something in the village that is genuinely hard to name — a coherence between how people live and the place they live in.

Getting There

Reaching villages like Ain Tissimane requires a car, some off-road capability, and ideally a local contact. The nearest larger town is about 45 minutes away on the main road. From there, unmarked tracks lead up into the mountains. I would not recommend going without a local guide — not because the region is unsafe, but because the tracks are confusing and GPS maps are often missing or wrong.

The best approach is to spend time in a smaller city first — Chefchaouen is an excellent base — and ask around. Many locals have family in the mountain villages and can make introductions. This is slow travel by necessity. You cannot pass through these places quickly. They require patience and a willingness to sit, eat slowly, and spend an evening watching the light change on the mountains.

I left Ain Tissimane on the fourth morning, before most of the village was awake. Ahmed's son walked me to the edge of the village and pointed me toward the main road. I gave him my email address on a piece of paper, knowing I would probably never hear from him. He thanked me with a formality that felt genuinely meant. The walk back to the car took an hour. The dust rose behind me as I drove away.
